      Consider lower and upper danboux sums:
      S_{\pi}[u] = \sum_{j=1}^{K(\pi)} m_{j}(t_{j} - t_{j-1}),  S^{\pi}[u] = \sum_{j=1}^{K(\pi)} M_{j}(t_{j} - t_{j-1})g
      NOTE: If we add point to the partion, then the lower sum may increase, and the upper sum may decrease.
      
      Def 11.6: A bounded function u:[a,b] \to R is said to be Reimann integrable if:
      \int_{*}u $= :$ \sup_{\pi}S_{\pi}[u] = \inf _{\pi}S^{\pi}[u] $: =$ \int^{*}u
      Where sup/inf is taken over all partioms \pi of [a,b].
      The common value of \int^{*}u and \int_{*}u is denoted by:
      (R)\int_{a}^{b}u(x)d(x) or \int_{a}^{b}u(x)dx


      Remark 11.7: One can easly find exambles of functions which are Lebesque int, but not R-intregrable.
      Let f:[0,1] \to defined by f(x)=1_{Q \cap [0,1]}(x), x \in [0,1]. 
      See that f is not (R)-int, but f is lebesque int.


      For any partion \pi og [0,1], we see that S_{\pi}[f]=0 and S^{\pi}[f]=1, wherefore f isn't Reimann integrable.
      What about the lebesque int? f is a nonneg, measurable since Q is countable = > Q \cap [0,1] is measurable.
      We see that $\int f d\lambda=\lambda (Q \cap [0,1])=0$.

      
      Remark: This is an instancw where one can se the advantage of Lebesque int vs Reimann int.
      Qustion: $(f_n)_{n \geq 1}$ is a sequence of non-neg integrable functions over [a,b], can we deduce that
      \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} is reimann-integrable. For Lebesque, the answer is YES. For Reiman, the answer in genereal is no
      UNLESS we asumme moreover that the sum converges uniformly, then the sum will be integrable.

      Theorem 11.8>
      Let u:[a,b] \to R be a measurable function. If u is Reimann integrable, then u is Lebesque integrablem and
      the integrals are equal to each other.
